Common causes of avalanches:

  • Clear avalanche activity. Do not go near slopes if you can see visible avalanche activity.
  • New snow build-up. 90% of all avalanches occur during snowstorms.
  • Stronger snow overlying weaker snow, also known as slab, lying on ice or compact snow.
  • Seemingly loose layers of snow, usually caused by loose graupel (rounded, heavily rimed pellets) or airspace.
  • Sudden increases in temperature. The higher the temperature, the less safe the situation becomes, even if snow doesn’t seem to thaw.

Steps to take in an avalanche:

If Caught:

  • If you have an ice axe, try plunging it into the surface under the moving snow. This might help to keep you near the top of the slide.
  • Shout. Others may hear and/or see you.
  • Try to run to the side, or jump up-slope, above the starting point of the avalanche.
  • Get rid of non-essential equipment.
  • Try to roll sideways off the debris.
  • Swimming motions sometimes help.
  • As the avalanche slows down, the debris might start to show some give. Try to get to the surface, or at least get a hand through.

If Buried:

  • Keep one hand in front of your face and try to keep a pocket for air.
  • Try to avoid compression of the chest area by taking and holding a deep breath to leave space for breathing.
  • Avoid panicking and save your energy. People are likely searching for you.

Avalanche Rescue:

Observe the victim's progress and if possible mark their point of entry and where they were last seen, and check for further avalanche danger.
Quickly search the debris surface:

  • Look for any victims.
  • Listen for any sounds.
  • Check the most likely burial spots.
  • Search systematically, probing debris with the appropriate tools.

Send for help, but keep searching until help arrives. Although survival chances decline rapidly with the duration of burial, they do not reach zero for a long time.
